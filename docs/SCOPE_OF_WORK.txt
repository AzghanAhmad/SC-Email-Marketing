ScribeCount Email II
Scope of Work (SOW)
ScribeCount Email Marketing Service for Independent Authors (SES-based, ScribeCount-integrated)
Document Version: vNext (Architecture & Integration Focus)
Client: ScribeCount
Service Name (Working): ScribeCount Email (powered by Amazon SES)
Target Users: Independent authors and small author teams
Primary Goal: Low-cost, high-impact email marketing system that produces data that merges cleanly with ScribeCount reporting

1) Executive Summary
ScribeCount will deliver a standalone email marketing service designed specifically for independent authors. The service will use Amazon SES as the sending backbone and will be engineered from day one to produce structured marketing and engagement data that integrates with the broader ScribeCount ecosystem—particularly website tracking, link/ad attribution, and sales reporting—to provide authors with actionable insights.
This service is not intended to be a generic marketing automation platform. It is intentionally optimized for how authors market books and related content and will exclude functions that authors do not routinely utilize at meaningful scale (notably SMS).
A core value proposition is that authors should be able to answer business-critical questions without assembling multiple tools or manually reconciling metrics. In practice: the email service will become one of ScribeCount’s primary “signal sources” that connects marketing actions to outcomes.

2) Objective (Expanded Narrative)
2.1 What the Feature Is
The feature is a full email marketing and automation system tailored to book marketing workflows. It includes:
Broadcast Campaigns (one-time or scheduled sends)
Flows / Automations (event-driven sequences for reader onboarding, launches, winback, etc.)
A Template System for emails and reusable content blocks (book cards, series order, etc.)
Audience management: Profiles, Lists, Segments, Tags, consent and suppression states
A deliverability system engineered around SES + SNS telemetry (bounce/complaint/delivery)
A reporting layer designed to join email performance to ScribeCount’s broader analytics
A Flow Template Library that includes premade flows with editable sample content, so authors can launch quickly without writing everything from scratch
2.2 How It Should Be Constructed
The system must be built with integration and maintainability as primary constraints. Implementation choices should prioritize:
Clear service boundaries (sending/deliverability vs orchestration vs reporting)
Strict event contracts that align with ScribeCount reporting needs
Versioned, auditable flows so authors and support teams can understand “why did this send”
Export pipelines that produce trusted, reconcilable data inside ScribeCount
Tooling for support and debug (audit trails, event traces, job histories, suppression rationales)
Feature-flag-driven rollouts (tested in Sandbox first)
This is a “ScribeCount-first” system: the email service is a standalone product in operation, but it is not a standalone source of truth. ScribeCount is the reporting hub, and the email system’s data must be shaped to join reliably with website and sales signals.
2.3 How Authors Will Use It
Authors do not want to build from scratch. They want to:
Set up sending once (domain + deliverability guardrails)
Install/enable a few proven automations (welcome/magnet, launch, post-purchase, review request)
Send periodic broadcasts (release updates, promos, behind-the-scenes)
Look at a dashboard that tells them what worked and what didn’t
The service must support an author’s core lifecycle:
List growth (forms/landing pages, reader magnets, segmentation)
Launch execution (preorder → launch day → post-launch sustain)
Backlist monetization (revivals, bundles, audio/print upgrades)
Direct sales lift (abandoned checkout, post-purchase upsell)
Engagement maintenance (surveys, winback, hygiene)
Authors should be able to install a premade flow template with sample content, edit it in minutes, connect it to their book/series link set, and activate it with confidence.
2.4 How the Data Integrates with ScribeCount to Produce Actionable Reports
The primary product outcome is not “emails sent,” but business clarity. The system must generate data that ScribeCount can combine with:
book release schedules and metadata
ad link attribution chains (ad → click → session → purchase)
website traffic signals
direct sales purchase events (when available)
retailer click-out performance (clearly labeled as directional where purchases cannot be confirmed)
The integrated reporting must enable practical decisions:
which flows increase sales or engagement
which segments are valuable for preorders
which retailers or direct channels respond best to the author’s audience
whether marketing effort correlates with revenue lift by title or series
how list growth quality changes over time (not just subscriber count)
2.5 Benefits
Benefits to Authors
Fast marketing execution through premade flows + editable content
Fewer tools to manage; reduced technical burden
Clear “what worked” signals instead of vanity metrics
Better launch consistency and backlist stability
Benefits to ScribeCount
Increased retention and stickiness (ScribeCount becomes the operating system)
Higher data completeness for attribution and performance reporting
Clear expansion path into AI optimization, template packs, premium analytics
Differentiated positioning: publishing-focused marketing intelligence, not generic e-commerce tooling

3) Guiding Principles and Constraints
3.1 Non-Negotiables
Amazon SES is the sending backbone.
No SMS is required in this scope.
Service functions as a standalone service but exports data ONLY to ScribeCount (besides necessary AWS SES/SNS delivery infrastructure).
All features must include tooltips, help icons, and information icons (centrally managed via a help registry).
Must support integration with the author’s website and direct sales platform.
3.2 Documentation Constraint (No Sample Code)
This SOW is intentionally architecture-first and does not include sample code. It may recommend construction patterns that support maintainability, but implementation details are left to engineering execution.
3.3 UI Reference Disclaimer (Non-Copy Requirement)
Any included screenshots or UI references from Author.Email and Klaviyo are provided for functional reference only (feature discoverability, workflows, terminology). They must not be copied verbatim, and no UI elements should be treated as design requirements. The final UI must be original and brand-aligned to ScribeCount.

5) Out of Scope
SMS messaging and SMS reporting
Building a full e-commerce product catalog engine comparable to large retail platforms
Guaranteed retailer purchase attribution where confirmed purchase data is not available
Copying UI designs from Author.Email or Klaviyo verbatim
External data export connectors beyond ScribeCount (explicitly prohibited)

6) Target Architecture (Conceptual)
6.1 Core Components
A) Sending & Deliverability Layer (SES)
responsible for sending, deliverability telemetry ingest, suppression actions
stores raw SNS payloads for audit/debug
produces normalized delivery events for reporting
B) Audience & Consent Layer
profiles, lists, segments, tags
consent logs and lifecycle state management
segment evaluation system (rules, previews, saved definitions)
C) Content Layer
templates and reusable blocks
asset library
link sets, UTM presets, link group classifications
D) Campaign Orchestration Layer
campaign lifecycle management
scheduling and send job creation
pre-flight checks and safety rails (suppression, domain readiness)
E) Flow Orchestration Layer
flow definitions and versions
runtime execution logs (“why did this send”)
node-level analytics and outcomes
F) Reporting & Analytics Layer
query-ready metrics tables (campaign/flow/link metrics)
deliverability and hygiene analytics
attribution join compatibility (email clicks → links → sessions → purchases)
G) ScribeCount Export Layer (Outbound to ScribeCount only)
export jobs, retries, reconciliation
auditing and scope controls
ensures ScribeCount is the unified reporting hub
6.2 Construction Guidance (No code, best-practice patterns)
Version everything that changes: flows, templates, campaign revisions
Event-first design: every action produces an event usable by reporting
Deterministic execution: flows should be traceable, explainable, and replayable for debugging
Separation of concerns: sending vs orchestration vs reporting should not be tightly coupled
Sandbox-first rollout: feature flags and seeded test tenants in Sandbox

7) Integration Requirements
7.1 Website Integration
The service must support collecting website events to enable:
list growth attribution (where subscribers came from)
conversion tracking (email click → session)
content performance reporting (landing page effectiveness)
Website integration should support:
installation guidance (wizard-driven)
event validation tooling (testing console)
event normalization into the shared ScribeCount event vocabulary
7.2 Direct Sales Platform Integration
The service must support integration with direct sales platforms such that:
purchases and cart events can trigger flows (post-purchase, abandoned checkout)
links and UTMs can join email performance to direct revenue outcomes
ScribeCount reporting can unify these signals
7.3 Link/Ad Attribution Integration
The email system must generate link metadata compatible with ScribeCount’s linking feature:
link group classification (Direct/Amazon/Kobo/etc.)
consistent UTM tagging
stable identifiers for joining click events to sessions/purchases
explicit labeling when downstream outcomes are directional/inferred
7.4 ScribeCount-Only Export Requirement
The email service must export only to ScribeCount:
performance metrics
subscriber lifecycle state changes
deliverability and suppression events
campaign and flow metadata required for reports
The export must support:
job history and retries
reconciliation to prevent drift
auditing for compliance and support investigations

8) Reporting Requirements (Actionable Data)
8.1 Minimum Reporting Views (V1)
Campaign performance dashboard

8.2 ScribeCount “Actionable Reports” (V1 outcomes)

11) Acceptance Criteria (High-Level)
A release is acceptable when:
SES sending is operational, and SNS events update suppression states correctly
Campaigns can be created, scheduled, sent, and reported reliably
Flows can be installed from templates, edited, versioned, activated, and traced
Reporting dashboards show consistent metrics with explainable definitions
Exports to ScribeCount are reliable, auditable, and reconcilable
No outbound exports exist beyond ScribeCount (besides SES/SNS infrastructure)
UI includes tooltips/help/info across all major controls

13) Risks and Mitigations
Deliverability Risk (new senders):
Mitigate via warmup patterns, throttles, hygiene defaults, deliverability guidance, and suppression transparency.
Attribution Ambiguity (retailers):
Mitigate by clearly labeling directional vs confirmed outcomes, and by designing reports to avoid implying certainty where it cannot exist.
Integration Drift:
Mitigate by strict data contracts, versioned schemas, and reconciliation jobs.
Support Burden:
Mitigate by audit logs, “why did this send” tracing, and raw SNS payload retention.

14) Future-Proofing (Explicitly Supported)
The system must be architected to support future ScribeCount enhancements without major refactors, including:
expanded attribution and ad cost imports
AI-assisted flow drafting and maintenance (draft-only guardrails)
deeper catalog/book metadata blocks in templates
enhanced cohort analytics and lifecycle segmentation
additional integrations that still preserve “ScribeCount-only export”
